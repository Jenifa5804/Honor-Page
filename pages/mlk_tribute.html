A Leader for Justice

Martin Luther King Jr. was an American Baptist minister and activist who became the most visible spokesperson and leader in the American civil rights movement from 1955 until his assassination in 1968. King advanced civil rights through nonviolence and civil disobedience, inspired by his Christian beliefs and the nonviolent activism of Mahatma Gandhi.

King led the 1955 Montgomery bus boycott and later became the first president of the Southern Christian Leadership Conference (SCLC). With the SCLC, he led an unsuccessful struggle against segregation in Albany, Georgia, and helped organize the nonviolent 1963 protests in Birmingham, Alabama. His leadership and powerful oratory skills helped bring about landmark legislation including the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Voting Rights Act of 1965.

"I Have a Dream" Speech

Delivered on August 28, 1963, during the March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom, this iconic speech articulated King's vision of racial harmony and equality. Spoken before 250,000 people at the Lincoln Memorial, it became one of the most influential speeches in American history.

"Darkness cannot drive out darkness; only light can do that. Hate cannot drive out hate; only love can do that."

— Martin Luther King Jr.

Eternal Legacy

Assassinated in Memphis, but his dream of equality continues to inspire movements worldwide

Martin Luther King Jr.'s legacy extends far beyond the civil rights movement. His philosophy of nonviolent resistance, his dream of equality, and his message of love over hate continue to inspire social justice movements around the world. His life reminds us that one person's courage and conviction can indeed change the course of history.
